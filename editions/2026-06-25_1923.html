01  The Week in Brief AI · Geopolitics · Tech · Funding

Five stories from the intersection of AI, geopolitics and the technology economy — curated and commented.

This week, the AI arms race moved from software into silicon, geopolitics, and the courtroom simultaneously. Anthropic accused Alibaba of illicitly extracting Claude's capabilities, OpenAI unveiled its first custom chip, and Europe pushed back on Washington's chip export controls — all against a backdrop of Micron posting a 15-fold profit surge that confirmed AI memory demand is reshaping the entire semiconductor industry. The competitive pressure on the leading labs has never been more visible, or more consequential.

Anthropic Accuses Alibaba of Illicitly Extracting Claude's Capabilities

— Financial Times

Anthropic has alleged that Alibaba used fake accounts to systematically extract the capabilities of its Claude AI model, in what amounts to a formal accusation of corporate IP theft against one of China's largest technology groups. The case marks one of the most direct public confrontations between a leading US AI lab and a Chinese tech giant over model misappropriation.

Why it matters

If the allegations hold, this sets a significant legal and diplomatic precedent for how AI model IP is protected — and enforced — across borders.

Tech context

Extracting a model's capabilities through fake accounts typically involves querying it extensively to replicate its behaviour in a rival system, a process known as model distillation or knowledge extraction — it does not require access to the underlying weights, making it difficult to prevent purely through contractual terms of service.

The London lens

UK AI labs operating under US-aligned export frameworks will be watching closely: a successful Anthropic legal argument could create new compliance obligations for any European model provider with Chinese enterprise customers.

OpenAI Unveils Its First Custom Chip, Built by Broadcom

— TechCrunch

OpenAI has revealed its first proprietary processor, named Jalapeño, designed in partnership with Broadcom and optimised specifically for inference workloads rather than training. The move signals OpenAI's intent to reduce its dependence on Nvidia and control more of its cost base as it approaches an IPO.

Why it matters

A custom inference chip gives OpenAI structural cost advantages at scale that pure Nvidia customers cannot match, reshaping the economics of AI deployment across the industry.

Tech context

Inference chips handle the task of running a trained model to generate outputs — distinct from training chips, which build the model in the first place. Optimising for inference rather than training allows for significantly lower power consumption and cost per query, which matters enormously at ChatGPT's volume.

Europe Is Pushing Back on Washington's Chip War

— TechCrunch

European governments and industry are resisting US pressure to extend semiconductor export controls to older-generation chip manufacturing tools, with ASML at the centre of the dispute. Washington's proposed MATCH Act would restrict sales of deep ultraviolet lithography machines — technology a decade old — that China can currently still legally purchase.

Why it matters

The outcome will determine whether Europe remains a rule-taker in US-led tech export policy or carves out independent space — with major commercial consequences for its most strategically important hardware company.

The London lens

The UK, which has aligned closely with Washington on export controls post-Brexit, faces a sharper version of this dilemma: diverging from the US risks diplomatic friction, while aligning fully risks stranding British chip equipment firms from European coordination.

Micron Posts 15-Fold Profit Surge as AI Memory Demand Hits Record

— Financial Times

Micron reported a 15-fold increase in quarterly profits, with revenue quadrupling year-on-year to $41.45 billion, driven almost entirely by insatiable demand for high-bandwidth memory used in AI systems. The results sent Micron's shares sharply higher and lifted Asian semiconductor markets, while Apple's shares fell after the company separately cited memory chip shortages as the reason for 20 per cent price rises on MacBooks and iPads.

Why it matters

Micron's results are a proxy for the entire AI infrastructure build-out: when a memory chipmaker posts numbers this extreme, it confirms that AI capex is still accelerating, not plateauing.

Tech context

High-bandwidth memory is a specialised chip architecture that stacks multiple memory dies vertically to achieve far faster data transfer rates than conventional DRAM — it is the critical component inside Nvidia's H100 and H200 GPUs, and supply constraints directly limit how many AI accelerators can be built.

General Intuition Raises $320M to Train AI Agents on Video Game Gameplay

— TechCrunch

General Intuition has raised $320 million at a $2.3 billion valuation on the thesis that millions of hours of video game action data can teach AI agents something closer to human intuition for navigating the real world. The company argues that games provide a uniquely rich, structured source of embodied decision-making data that text-based training cannot replicate.

Why it matters

If game-derived training genuinely transfers to real-world agent behaviour, it opens a vast and largely untapped data category for AI development at a moment when high-quality training data is becoming a key competitive constraint.

Tech context

Most current AI agents are trained on text or synthetic data, which captures knowledge but struggles to encode the rapid, sequential, consequence-aware decision-making that physical and interactive environments demand. Video games offer billions of labelled state-action pairs in environments with clear rules and feedback — a structural advantage for teaching agents to act, not just reason.

Watch This

Qualcomm landing Meta as its first major data centre chip customer is easy to dismiss as a minor design win, but if Meta's AI infrastructure team validates Qualcomm's server silicon at scale, it could be the first credible crack in Nvidia's near-monopoly on AI data centre compute — and that dynamic could move very fast in the second half of 2026.

02  Minds Worth Following Essays · Long reads · Podcasts

The sharpest thing written, said or published this week — on AI, ideas, or anything worth thinking about.

Financial Times

What If Ideas Aren't Getting Harder to Find, After All?

The piece challenges the widely held economic assumption that innovation is decelerating because low-hanging fruit has been picked — arguing instead that large companies have simply become more effective at internalising the gains from R&D while making those gains invisible to outside measurement. If true, this reframes the AI productivity debate entirely: the problem may not be that AI is failing to generate ideas, but that we have always been bad at seeing where ideas actually go.

Financial Times

How Much Compute Does the World Really Need?

This piece argues that simply scaling compute cannot resolve AI's core accuracy problem — that more processing power produces bigger, more confident models, but does not make them more reliably correct. The central insight is that the industry has conflated capability with accuracy, and that the trillion-dollar infrastructure build-out currently underway may be solving the wrong problem at extraordinary expense.

Financial Times

Why Aren't More Companies Adopting AI?

Despite the volume of AI investment and the maturity of available tools, enterprise adoption remains surprisingly shallow — and this piece argues the barrier is not technical but organisational. The real constraint is that most companies lack the internal structures, incentive systems, and change management capacity to integrate AI into existing workflows, meaning the technology is sitting above the organisation rather than inside it. For any senior leader frustrated by the gap between AI pilots and AI at scale, this is a useful diagnostic.

Quote of the week

“Scale cannot solve AI's fundamental problem with accuracy. The industry has spent a decade assuming that more compute produces better outcomes — but what it actually produces is more confident outcomes, which is a very different thing.”

— Financial Times analysis, 'How Much Compute Does the World Really Need?'
03  Staying Human Body · Mind · The Analogue World

Your health, your attention, your life off the screen. Evidence-led and never preachy.

The Practice

The Case for Deliberate Recovery Windows During the Working Day

NHS England's data on Palantir's £330 million contract this week admitted it cannot attribute measurable performance improvements to the system — a reminder that technology does not automatically improve human output. The underlying research on cognitive performance is more instructive: repeated studies show that focused work degrades significantly after 90 minutes without a genuine recovery break, and that the quality of that break matters as much as its length. A short walk, eyes off screens, and no task-switching for 10–15 minutes restores prefrontal function more effectively than passive scrolling. For professionals in back-to-back meeting cultures, scheduling two deliberate recovery windows before 3pm — not lunch, not a coffee catch-up, but actual cognitive rest — produces measurably better decision quality in the afternoon.

Via Journal of Cognitive Enhancement  ·  Andrew Huberman, Stanford Neurobiology
The Edit
China's Humanoid Robot Bet Is a Labour Policy, Not a Tech Story

China's workforce is projected to fall from roughly 900 million today to 300 million by the end of the century, and Beijing's humanoid robotics push is best understood as a demographic survival strategy rather than a technology competition. The piece makes a compelling case that China's structural labour crisis — not AI ambition — is the actual driver behind why it now manufactures the majority of the world's industrial robots.

How AI Is Transforming Natural Disaster Prediction

Catastrophe modellers are using AI to move beyond the hard limits of physics-based simulations, which struggle with rare, high-impact events precisely because historical data for them is thin — the piece explains how probabilistic AI models are beginning to fill that gap in ways that are materially changing how insurers price climate and disaster risk.

What Ambani's Deep-Tech Ambitions Reveal About India's AI Limits

Despite Reliance's scale and Ambani's stated ambitions, India still lacks the semiconductor manufacturing base, the domestic chip design talent, and the sovereign cloud infrastructure to be genuinely self-reliant in AI — and this piece argues that without addressing those foundations, India risks becoming a large consumer of AI built entirely by others rather than a genuine producer.
