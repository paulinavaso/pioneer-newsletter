01  The Week in Brief AI · Geopolitics · Tech · Funding

Five stories from the intersection of AI, geopolitics and the technology economy — curated and commented.

This week the politics of AI hardened into something structural. The US government screened access to OpenAI's latest model, Anthropic's Mythos sat at the centre of a federal-versus-state standoff, and South Korean chipmakers committed $600bn to head off a global memory shortage — all in the same seven days. The question is no longer whether AI shapes geopolitics; it is whether governments can govern AI fast enough to keep up with it.

OpenAI limits GPT-5.6 rollout after US government request

— TechCrunch

OpenAI released GPT-5.6 — described as carrying powerful cyber security capabilities — only to users vetted by the US government, following a direct request from Washington. OpenAI made clear it views this as an unwelcome precedent, stating the arrangement keeps its best tools from developers, enterprises, and international partners.

Why it matters

A major AI lab being forced to gate its most capable model by government fiat signals a new era of state-directed AI deployment that has no established rulebook and significant consequences for global competitiveness.

The London lens

UK enterprises and cyber defenders are among the 'global partners' OpenAI says are being shut out — a concrete reminder that Washington's ad hoc access controls have immediate costs for allies who assumed open markets.

Anthropic's Mythos becomes a federal-versus-state battleground

— TechCrunch

The Trump administration authorised more than 100 US companies and agencies to use Anthropic's Mythos 5, while simultaneously restricting broader access — creating tension with Anthropic, which separately struck a deal to give California state government half-price access to Claude. The divergence places Anthropic between a hostile federal government and a sympathetic state partner.

Why it matters

AI capability is now a site of active conflict between US federal and state governments, and the lab caught in the middle must navigate both relationships simultaneously — a dynamic that will shape how AI governance fractures across other democracies.

Tech context

Mythos is Anthropic's most capable model tier, built for high-stakes applications including intelligence and defence work; restricting its export or distribution does not just limit business, it determines which institutions globally can access frontier reasoning tools.

Samsung and SK Hynix commit $600bn to avert 'RAMageddon'

— TechCrunch

Samsung and SK Hynix, the world's two largest memory chip producers, announced plans to invest more than $600bn alongside the South Korean government to expand memory chip manufacturing capacity. The move is a direct response to surging AI demand that has pushed memory supply to its limits — a situation the industry is calling 'RAMageddon'.

Why it matters

Memory chips are the binding constraint on how fast AI infrastructure can scale; this is the largest single industrial commitment to resolving that bottleneck, and it will determine the pace of AI deployment globally for the next decade.

Tech context

High-bandwidth memory (HBM) — the specialised chip that sits alongside AI processors and feeds them data at extreme speed — is the scarce resource at issue. SK Hynix is currently the dominant supplier; Samsung is racing to close the gap, and both are betting that demand will vastly outpace current fab capacity.

Asian AI startups fill the vacuum left by Anthropic's export ban

— TechCrunch

As Anthropic's most capable model remains unavailable across large parts of Asia due to US export restrictions, regional startups are launching frontier-class alternatives that promise comparable capability without the geopolitical strings. The piece argues US AI labs may permanently lose Asian enterprise markets they will never recover.

Why it matters

Export controls designed to maintain US AI supremacy are accelerating the emergence of competitive non-US frontier models — the precise outcome they were meant to prevent.

Google caps Meta's access to Gemini as compute scarcity bites

— Financial Times

Google has placed capacity limits on Meta's use of its Gemini models, as surging demand for advanced AI pushes available computing power to its limits across the industry. The episode illustrates that even the largest technology companies now face hard constraints on AI access — compute has become the tech industry's scarcest commodity.

Why it matters

When one hyperscaler must ration AI access to another, the compute shortage is no longer theoretical — it is actively reshaping commercial relationships and competitive dynamics at the very top of the industry.

The London lens

UK AI startups and scaleups that rely on US hyperscaler APIs for their products are directly exposed to rationing decisions made in Mountain View or Seattle; the case for building on distributed alternatives or European infrastructure has never been stronger.

Watch This

IBM's new prototype chip — packing 100 billion transistors on an area the size of a fingernail — could extend Moore's Law another decade; if it moves from prototype to production, it reshapes every assumption about future compute costs and AI hardware roadmaps.

02  Minds Worth Following Essays · Long reads · Podcasts

The sharpest thing written, said or published this week — on AI, ideas, or anything worth thinking about.

Financial Times

Artificial Intelligence and Engels' Pause

The piece draws on the Victorian concept of 'Engels' Pause' — the decades-long lag between the arrival of industrial technology and any broad improvement in living standards — to argue that AI's most significant constraint may not be technical but political. The argument is that societies struggling with inequality, distrust, and institutional dysfunction will block or distort AI's benefits long before power shortages or compute limits become binding. For professionals trying to anticipate where AI deployment actually stalls, this is the more useful frame.

MIT Technology Review

AI Agents Are Not Your Coworkers

The piece takes direct aim at the corporate habit of giving AI agents human names, pronouns, and colleague-like status — arguing it is not a harmless UX choice but a deliberate framing designed to obscure accountability and encourage users to over-trust systems that have no understanding, no judgment, and no stake in outcomes. The central insight is that anthropomorphising agents shifts the burden of responsibility from the organisation deploying them onto the individual user who was told they had a 'teammate'. It is a short read that will change how you hear the next enterprise AI pitch.

MIT Technology Review

Heat Waves Mess with Your Brain. Scientists Are Trying to Figure Out Why.

Written during the week London recorded its highest ever June temperature, this piece surveys the emerging research on how sustained heat impairs cognitive function — affecting attention, memory consolidation, and decision-making — at temperatures many professionals would not consider dangerous. The science suggests the productivity cost of heatwaves is substantially underestimated because most studies measure physical health outcomes, not the subtler cognitive degradation that hits before anyone feels unwell. Directly relevant to anyone who worked through this week's heat and wondered why their thinking felt slower.

03  Staying Human Body · Mind · The Analogue World

Your health, your attention, your life off the screen. Evidence-led and never preachy.

The Practice

Protect your cognition during heatwaves — it degrades before you notice

Research reviewed this week found that sustained heat impairs attention, working memory, and decision-making at temperatures most people do not flag as dangerous — the cognitive effects emerge well before physical symptoms of heat stress. The mechanism involves disrupted sleep, elevated cortisol, and reduced cerebral blood flow, all of which compound across consecutive hot days. For anyone in a senior role making consequential decisions, the practical implication is to front-load cognitively demanding work to the coolest part of the day, prioritise sleep quality even if total sleep hours feel adequate, and treat a multi-day heatwave as a genuine performance risk rather than a comfort inconvenience. Hydration and cooled sleeping environments have the strongest evidence base for preserving cognitive function specifically, as opposed to general heat safety.

Via MIT Technology Review  ·  Jessica Hamzelou, MIT Technology Review
The Edit
The UK Tech Boom Beyond London

The piece makes the underreported case that UK tech is now expanding faster than financial services, and that the real competition for talent and capital is not London versus New York but London versus Manchester, Bristol, and Edinburgh — cities actively building the infrastructure to pull investment out of the capital. If you work in or fund UK tech and still think of it as a London story, this will update your map.

Ford Rehires the Engineers AI Was Supposed to Replace

Ford brought back experienced engineers after discovering that deploying AI into its manufacturing process without their domain expertise produced worse outcomes, not better — a frank admission that 'just adding AI' is not a substitute for embedded institutional knowledge. The lesson travels well beyond automotive: AI augments expert judgment, it does not yet replace the judgment itself.

Sony Erases Your Digital Library — A Reminder You Own Nothing

Sony's removal of purchased digital content from customer libraries is the clearest recent demonstration of a structural problem with digital ownership: a licence to access is not ownership, and the distinction only becomes visible when the company withdraws it. As more of professional and personal life moves into licensed digital infrastructure — software, media, data — the question of what you actually control becomes material.
