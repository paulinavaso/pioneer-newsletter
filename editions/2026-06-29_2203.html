01  The Week in Brief AI · Geopolitics · Tech · Funding

Five stories from the intersection of AI, geopolitics and the technology economy — curated and commented.

This week, the AI industry collided hard with political reality. The US government restricted OpenAI's most powerful model at launch, Anthropic's Mythos navigated a patchwork of access deals, and Asian rivals are quietly filling the gaps left by US export controls. Meanwhile, a $600bn memory chip expansion and a warning from the Bank for International Settlements about AI 'exuberance' framed the same question from opposite ends: is this boom built on solid ground?

OpenAI restricted GPT-5.6 at launch after government request — and said it shouldn't happen again

— TechCrunch

OpenAI released GPT-5.6 to a select group of government-vetted users rather than the public, following a direct request from the Trump administration citing the model's advanced cyber security capabilities. The company complied but issued a pointed statement saying this kind of government access gate should not become standard practice.

Why it matters

It establishes a precedent — contested by OpenAI itself — in which a government can delay or restrict a commercial AI release on national security grounds, with profound implications for how frontier models reach the rest of the world.

The London lens

The UK has no equivalent gating mechanism for frontier AI releases. If Washington normalises pre-release government vetting, British enterprises, developers, and security agencies could find themselves systematically behind American counterparts in accessing the most capable tools.

Asian AI startups are filling the Mythos-shaped gap left by US export controls

— TechCrunch

While Anthropic's Mythos model remains export-restricted from much of Asia, a wave of Asian AI startups has launched models that match its claimed capabilities and carry no access restrictions. The article argues that US AI labs may never recover the enormous Asian market if the export ban continues.

Why it matters

US export controls, designed to preserve a technological edge, may be accelerating the very competitive threat they were meant to prevent by handing Asian markets to domestic alternatives.

Tech context

Mythos refers to Anthropic's most powerful model class. Export restrictions mean non-US entities in certain jurisdictions cannot legally access or build on it, creating a commercial vacuum that local competitors are now moving to fill.

Samsung and SK Hynix commit $600bn to memory chip expansion as AI demand strains supply

— TechCrunch

Samsung and SK Hynix, the world's two largest memory chip makers, have announced a combined investment of over $550bn — alongside South Korean government co-funding — to build new fabrication facilities. The expansion is a direct response to what industry insiders are calling 'RAMageddon': a critical shortage of high-bandwidth memory needed to run large AI models.

Why it matters

Memory scarcity, not compute alone, is now a principal bottleneck for AI development, and this expansion will determine the pace at which AI infrastructure can scale globally over the next five years.

Tech context

High-bandwidth memory sits directly on or beside AI accelerator chips and feeds them data at speeds that conventional DRAM cannot match. Every major AI training and inference chip requires it, and demand has far outpaced the industry's ability to build new production capacity.

BIS warns AI 'exuberance' risks a prolonged investment bust

— Financial Times

The Bank for International Settlements has warned that weak returns on AI investment could trigger a sharp pullback in tech funding severe enough to threaten the global economy. The BIS drew parallels with previous technology investment cycles and said current valuations reflect enthusiasm rather than demonstrated productivity gains.

Why it matters

When the central bank of central banks issues a systemic risk warning about a single technology sector, it signals that the AI spending cycle has grown large enough to carry macro-level downside risk if sentiment turns.

The London lens

London's position as Europe's leading AI hub makes it acutely exposed to any funding contraction: a significant share of UK AI venture activity is tied to US institutional capital that would retreat fastest in a risk-off environment.

Google capped Meta's access to Gemini as compute scarcity bites

— Financial Times

Google has placed usage limits on Meta's access to its Gemini models as surging demand for advanced AI pushes computing capacity to its limits. The incident illustrates how access to frontier AI is increasingly rationed by infrastructure constraints rather than commercial willingness.

Why it matters

When one of the world's most valuable companies cannot get unimpeded access to a competitor's AI model, it confirms that compute scarcity — not talent or capital — is now the primary constraint on AI deployment at scale.

Watch This

OpenAI's Jalapeño custom inference chip, built with Broadcom, is still in early rollout — but if it performs at production scale, it could meaningfully reduce OpenAI's dependence on Nvidia and compress the margins Nvidia currently extracts from every major AI lab.

·

Arena, the AI model leaderboard used by researchers worldwide to rank model quality, has just crossed $100m in revenue from a commercial service launched only nine months ago — watch whether it becomes the de facto procurement signal for enterprise AI buyers, effectively giving a single startup outsized influence over which models win adoption.

02  Minds Worth Following Essays · Long reads · Podcasts

The sharpest thing written, said or published this week — on AI, ideas, or anything worth thinking about.

MIT Technology Review

AI Agents Are Not Your Coworkers

The piece argues that the corporate habit of giving AI agents human names and 'colleague' framing is not just a marketing quirk but a deliberate design choice that shapes how workers relate to these tools — and how accountability gets diffused when things go wrong. By naming an AI agent 'Alex' and placing it in a reporting structure, organisations import the emotional and ethical assumptions of human employment into a context where none of those protections or obligations apply. The author's central concern is that this anthropomorphism makes it harder, not easier, to think clearly about what AI agents actually are and who is responsible for their outputs.

Financial Times

Artificial Intelligence and Engels' Pause

The essay borrows the concept of 'Engels' Pause' — the period during the Industrial Revolution when productivity surged but living standards for workers stagnated for decades — and applies it to AI. The argument is that politics, not power or compute, will be the real constraint on how AI's gains are distributed: who captures the productivity upside, how quickly, and whether democratic institutions can respond fast enough to prevent a long period of disruption without corresponding social benefit. It is a useful corrective to purely technical framings of the AI transition.

MIT Technology Review

Heat Waves Mess With Your Brain. Scientists Are Trying to Figure Out Why.

Following the UK's record June temperatures this week, this piece examines the emerging science on how extreme heat impairs cognitive function — slowing reaction times, degrading decision-making, and disrupting sleep — in ways that go well beyond simple discomfort. Researchers are finding that the brain is surprisingly vulnerable to even modest temperature increases and that the cognitive costs of heatwaves may be systematically underestimated because they are diffuse and hard to attribute. For anyone who noticed they were less sharp this week, the science here is both validating and genuinely alarming.

03  Staying Human Body · Mind · The Analogue World

Your health, your attention, your life off the screen. Evidence-led and never preachy.

The Practice

What extreme heat does to your brain — and how to protect it

This week's European heatwave coincided with new research findings confirming that even moderate heat exposure impairs cognitive performance: reaction times slow, working memory degrades, and decision quality drops before you feel acutely unwell. The mechanism appears to involve the brain's thermal regulation drawing resources away from prefrontal function — the part of the brain responsible for complex reasoning and impulse control. For professionals doing demanding cognitive work, the practical implication is clear: the quality of decisions made in unventilated offices or during commutes in 36°C heat is measurably worse, not just subjectively harder. Prioritising cool environments during the core working hours of a heatwave is not comfort-seeking — it is performance management.

Via MIT Technology Review  ·  Jessica Hamzelou, MIT Technology Review
The Edit
Tech: The UK's Big Growth Hope

The UK tech sector is now growing far faster than financial services, and the story of where that growth is actually happening — increasingly outside London, in cities that are quietly building real clusters — is more interesting and more politically significant than the headline number suggests.

Robots, Not Chatbots, Will Realise AI's Potential

The essay argues that the productivity gains most commonly promised from AI will not arrive through language models answering questions, but through physical robots operating on factory floors — and that rich-world economies are only beginning to understand what that transition will actually require in terms of infrastructure, retraining, and industrial policy.

The New AI-Based World Order

The piece contends that AI capability — specifically, which nations and companies control the most powerful models and the infrastructure to run them — is now the single dominant factor determining the hierarchy of global economic returns, displacing the energy, finance, and manufacturing advantages that shaped the previous world order.
