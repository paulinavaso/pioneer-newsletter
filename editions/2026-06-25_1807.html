01  The Week in Brief AI · Geopolitics · Tech · Funding

Five stories from the intersection of AI, geopolitics and the technology economy — curated and commented.

The US export ban on Anthropic's most advanced AI models is reshaping the global AI order faster than anyone anticipated, pushing allies toward sovereign AI strategies and handing China a propaganda win just as Beijing claimed the supercomputing crown without a single American chip. Meanwhile, the talent war at the frontier intensifies: Google DeepMind is haemorrhaging senior researchers to Anthropic, which is simultaneously being sued by a startup denied access to its own restricted models. The week's throughline is control — of compute, of talent, of the rules governing who gets access to the most powerful technology on earth.

US export ban on Anthropic's AI models fractures alliances and triggers sovereign AI race

— Al Jazeera English

The Trump administration has blocked allied nations from accessing Anthropic's most advanced AI models, including Claude Mythos and Fable 5, prompting governments worldwide to accelerate plans to build independent AI capabilities. The move has been condemned in some quarters as a strategic own goal, cutting off partners who might otherwise anchor themselves to American AI infrastructure.

Why it matters

When the US restricts AI access to its own allies, it doesn't neutralise those allies — it pushes them to build competing systems or turn to Chinese alternatives, fracturing the very coalition needed to set global AI norms.

The London lens

Britain was explicitly cut off from Anthropic's most powerful models last week, with one commentator in the Daily Mail describing it as being 'consigned to a future as a defenceless, third-rate power' — an overstatement, but one that reflects genuine alarm in UK national security circles about dependency on US AI infrastructure.

China tops the global supercomputer rankings for the first time since 2017 — using no US chips

— The Next Web

A supercomputer in Shenzhen has claimed the top spot in the global Top500 rankings, the first Chinese system to do so in nearly a decade. The machine runs entirely on domestically produced silicon, with no components from Nvidia, AMD, or Intel — a direct rebuttal to the thesis that US export controls are containing China's compute ambitions.

Why it matters

Supercomputing leadership is a proxy for AI and military capability; the fact that China achieved it on homegrown chips while under sanctions suggests export controls are accelerating Chinese self-sufficiency rather than halting it.

Tech context

The Top500 list ranks the world's most powerful supercomputers by a benchmark called LINPACK, which measures how fast a system solves a dense system of linear equations — a reasonable proxy for the kind of parallel computation used in AI training. Reaching the top without US chips means China has closed a gap that Western policymakers assumed would take much longer.

Google DeepMind loses Nobel laureate John Jumper to Anthropic as researcher exodus deepens

— Fortune

John Jumper, the Nobel Prize-winning scientist behind AlphaFold, has left Google DeepMind for Anthropic — the third senior departure from the lab in as many months. With DeepMind's models slipping down AI leaderboards and its pace of releases trailing competitors, questions are mounting about whether Alphabet can remain at the frontier.

Why it matters

Talent concentration at two or three labs is the clearest leading indicator of where AI breakthroughs will happen next; when a Nobel laureate votes with his feet, the direction of travel matters as much as the individual move.

The London lens

Google DeepMind's headquarters is in London's King's Cross, making it one of the UK's most important technology assets; sustained senior departures raise legitimate questions about whether the lab's London base will remain a centre of gravity for frontier AI research or become a support office for models built elsewhere.

Groq raises $650 million to rebuild as an AI inference cloud after Nvidia acquired its founder

— The Next Web

Groq has confirmed a $650 million fundraise to reposition itself as an AI inference cloud business, six months after Nvidia paid out its investors and hired away its founder. The round is a bet that purpose-built inference chips can outperform general-purpose GPUs for the specific task of running AI models at scale.

Why it matters

The AI infrastructure layer is being contested from multiple directions; if purpose-built inference hardware can undercut Nvidia on cost and speed for deployment workloads, it changes the economics of running AI at enterprise scale.

Tech context

Inference is the process of running a trained AI model to generate outputs — as distinct from training, which builds the model in the first place. Most commercial AI spending is now on inference, not training, which is why chips optimised for that narrower task can compete even against Nvidia's dominant general-purpose GPUs.

DeepSeek bans investor poaching as it closes a $7.4 billion funding round

— Crypto Briefing

Chinese AI lab DeepSeek has closed what would be its first major external fundraise at a reported $7.4 billion valuation, with founder Liang Wenfeng telling prospective investors during a four-hour virtual meeting that a strict no-poaching condition applies to its staff. The stipulation reflects how acutely DeepSeek views its team as its primary strategic asset.

Why it matters

DeepSeek moving from a self-funded research operation to institutionally backed company marks a structural shift in the China AI landscape — and the no-poaching clause signals that the global talent war has reached a point where labs are negotiating staff retention terms with investors before accepting their money.

Watch This

China is tightening export scrutiny on indium, a metal critical to high-speed AI chip manufacturing — if formal restrictions follow, it could hit global chip production timelines in ways that make the current Nvidia supply crunch look manageable by comparison.

02  Minds Worth Following Essays · Long reads · Podcasts

The sharpest thing written, said or published this week — on AI, ideas, or anything worth thinking about.

Vox

Who gave AI companies the right to build the future?

Using Oppenheimer's post-war security hearings as a frame, this piece asks whether AI labs have assumed a mandate they were never given — to shape civilisation-scale technology with minimal democratic accountability. The central argument is that the 'safety-focused' self-image of labs like Anthropic sits uneasily alongside their active lobbying for export controls that entrench their own market position. It is one of the sharper pieces of structural criticism written about the AI industry this year.

Tim.blog

Sebastian Mallaby on the race to superintelligence, the religion of AI, and spotting breakthroughs early

Mallaby, biographer of Demis Hassabis and a Pulitzer finalist, synthesises more than 100 interviews with AI insiders into a portrait of an industry that increasingly resembles a secular religion — one with its own eschatology, priesthood, and schisms. His sharpest insight is that the people most likely to build transformative AI are often the least equipped, temperamentally, to think carefully about what happens after they do. For anyone trying to understand the internal culture of frontier labs, this is the most substantive long-form account available this week.

Harvard Business Review

Employees aren't questioning AI advice enough

A new study finds that workers frequently avoid seeking additional information when doing so might complicate an AI-assisted decision or create moral discomfort — a behaviour the researchers call 'wilful ignorance.' The implication is not that AI gives bad advice, but that the social dynamics around AI use actively discourage the scrutiny that would catch the cases when it does. For senior professionals who sign off on AI-assisted analysis, this is the most practically relevant research published this week.

Quote of the week

“The people most capable of building transformative AI are often the least temperamentally suited to think carefully about what comes after they build it.”

— Sebastian Mallaby, author and Paul A. Volcker Senior Fellow, Council on Foreign Relations
03  Staying Human Body · Mind · The Analogue World

Your health, your attention, your life off the screen. Evidence-led and never preachy.

The Practice

The case for deliberate information-seeking before acting on AI outputs

New research published in Harvard Business Review finds that professionals systematically avoid seeking additional information when it might complicate a decision already supported by AI — particularly when moral stakes are involved. The mechanism is familiar to anyone who has felt the pull of a clean AI summary: once a plausible answer exists, the cost of complicating it feels higher than the benefit of being right. For senior professionals, the practical fix is structural rather than motivational: before acting on any AI-assisted recommendation, nominate a specific piece of contradicting evidence you would need to see to change your mind, and go looking for it. This simple pre-commitment breaks the passive acceptance loop the research identifies, and takes under two minutes.

The Edit
The White House Is Making Up Its AI Rules in Real Time

Wired reports that Anthropic still cannot distribute its most powerful models after falling foul of Trump administration export rules — but no official can articulate precisely what the company did wrong. The piece reveals that the regulatory framework governing the most consequential technology on earth is being improvised, with enforcement preceding any coherent written standard.

G7 AI talks expose the trust gap behind US model power

Digitimes reports from the G7 AI summit in France, where OpenAI and Anthropic arrived with a message about the need for global rules — while simultaneously benefiting from US policies that ensure those rules are written in Washington. The piece is a concise account of how frontier AI governance is becoming an extension of US strategic competition rather than a genuinely multilateral project.

Google DeepMind is giving A24 $75 million to build AI filmmaking tools

DeepMind's partnership with A24 — the indie studio behind Everything Everywhere All at Once and The Zone of Interest — is the most interesting creative-tech deal of the week: rather than pursuing a major studio, Google has chosen the house with the most distinctive aesthetic identity, betting that credible creative cover is worth more than raw distribution reach.
