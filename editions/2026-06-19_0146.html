01  The Week in Brief AI · Geopolitics · Tech · Funding

Five stories from the intersection of AI, geopolitics and the technology economy — curated and commented.

This week the tension between AI's global reach and national sovereignty moved from abstract to operational: world leaders at the G7 demanded American AI without American kill switches, while SpaceX's record IPO handed Elon Musk an acquisition war chest that is reshaping how power concentrates in tech. Beneath both stories runs the same question — who controls the infrastructure of the next decade?

World leaders want American AI. They just don't want America to be able to turn it off.

— TechCrunch

At the G7 summit, French President Macron and Indian Prime Minister Modi raised explicit concerns that the United States could cut off access to American AI systems overnight — a fear made concrete by a recent Anthropic service blackout. The episode has accelerated calls for sovereign AI infrastructure and alternatives to US-controlled models.

Why it matters

AI dependency is becoming a geopolitical fault line: allies are now treating access to American AI with the same strategic anxiety they once reserved for energy supply.

The London lens

The UK sits in an uncomfortable middle position — deeply integrated with US AI providers through the NIST framework and bilateral tech agreements, yet pushing its own AI Safety Institute agenda. This debate will force a clearer choice about whether Britain builds sovereign capacity or accepts structural dependency.

Trump is taking a page out of China's sovereign AI playbook

— Financial Times

Governments have historically protected strategic industries through tariffs and subsidies, but the US is now moving toward taking direct equity stakes in AI companies — a model China pioneered. The shift marks a fundamental change in how Western governments relate to technology: from regulator to co-owner.

Why it matters

State capitalism is arriving in Silicon Valley, and the competitive implications for European AI policy — which has relied on regulation rather than investment — are significant.

The London lens

The UK government's existing stakes in companies like OneWeb and its ARM interventions suggest some appetite for this model, but Britain lacks both the capital and the political consensus to match US or Chinese scale.

Anthropic chief tells G7 leaders to 'resist the temptation to splinter' over AI

— Financial Times

Dario Amodei addressed G7 leaders with a call for international cooperation on AI governance, warning that fragmentation of AI standards across blocs would be worse than imperfect global agreement. Sam Altman offered public support for the position, marking a rare moment of unity between the two most powerful AI lab chiefs.

Why it matters

When the heads of the world's two leading AI labs align on a geopolitical message at a G7 summit, it signals that frontier AI firms are now functioning as quasi-diplomatic actors in their own right.

Amazon backs AI start-up developing models to simulate physical world

— Financial Times

Odyssey, which builds world models — AI systems that simulate physical environments rather than just generating text or images — has raised $310 million from Amazon, Nvidia, and AMD at a $1.45 billion valuation. The round signals that the biggest infrastructure players are betting heavily on the next generation of AI beyond large language models.

Why it matters

World models are foundational to robotics, autonomous vehicles, and physical AI; the convergence of three major chip and cloud players into one round suggests a land-grab is underway for the infrastructure layer beneath consumer AI.

Tech context

A world model is an AI system trained to predict how physical environments evolve over time — think of it as a simulator of reality rather than a text predictor. It enables machines to plan actions in the real world, which is why robotics and autonomous systems companies need them.

AI inference startup Baseten reportedly raising $1.5B months after its last mega-round

— TechCrunch

Baseten, which provides infrastructure for running AI models in production rather than training them, is reportedly close to closing a $1.5 billion round at a $13 billion valuation — just months after its previous large raise. The deal reflects surging enterprise demand for reliable, scalable inference capacity as more companies deploy AI into live products.

Why it matters

The inference gold rush is producing valuations that rival model developers themselves, confirming that the monetisable bottleneck in enterprise AI has shifted from building models to running them at scale.

Tech context

Inference is what happens when a trained AI model is actually used — answering a question, generating an image, processing a document. It is computationally intensive and increasingly expensive at scale, which is why companies specialising in inference infrastructure are attracting capital at the same rate as model builders.

Watch This

Amazon's move to sell its Trainium chips externally to other data centres is a small announcement this week, but if AWS captures even a fraction of the third-party chip market it could meaningfully erode Nvidia's monopoly on AI infrastructure within 18 months.

02  Minds Worth Following Essays · Long reads · Podcasts

The sharpest thing written, said or published this week — on AI, ideas, or anything worth thinking about.

Financial Times

How AI is 'senior-ising' junior roles

The piece argues that AI is not simply automating junior tasks but compressing the career ladder: employers now expect new recruits to operate at the level of a mid-career professional from day one, taking on managerial judgment and decision-making that previously required years of accumulated experience. The implication is that the apprenticeship model — learning by doing routine work under seniors — is breaking down before anything has replaced it. For organisations, this creates a hidden risk: a generation of workers who can appear capable while lacking the foundational exposure that builds genuine expertise.

Financial Times

Tech's private subsea cables are a threat to everyone else

American hyperscalers — Google, Meta, Microsoft, Amazon — now own or co-own a significant portion of the world's undersea data cable network, the physical backbone of global internet traffic. The piece argues this creates a structural national security risk for every non-US country: a private company operating under US jurisdiction controls infrastructure that governments cannot easily access, reroute, or protect. The analysis lands at a moment when cable sabotage in the Baltic and elsewhere has already moved subsea infrastructure from an obscure technical concern to a live geopolitical one.

Financial Times

To avoid backlash, tech giants must share their AI wealth before it's too late

The argument is straightforward and deliberately uncomfortable: AI-driven productivity gains are accumulating at the top of the income distribution faster than any previous technological shift, and the political window for voluntary redistribution is closing. The piece contends that waiting for the worst job displacement to materialise before acting on corporate and public policy is not caution — it is a choice to let the backlash build until it becomes ungovernable. The implication for tech leadership is that pre-emptive profit-sharing, worker equity stakes, and retraining investment are not charity but political insurance.

Quote of the week

“The United States cannot wait for the worst job losses to hit before moving corporate and public policy in a pro-worker direction. By then, the political window will have closed.”

— Financial Times editorial, 17 June 2026
03  Staying Human Body · Mind · The Analogue World

Your health, your attention, your life off the screen. Evidence-led and never preachy.

The Practice

AI diagnostic tools are now matching doctors — here is what that means for how you use healthcare

Two peer-reviewed studies this week found that AI medical models matched or outperformed clinicians across a range of diagnostic and treatment decisions. This is not a future projection — it is a present reality that most patients have no framework for using. The practical implication for anyone with a demanding professional life is that AI-assisted second opinions are now clinically defensible: using a medically-trained model to cross-check a diagnosis or treatment plan before accepting it is no longer fringe behaviour. The studies do not suggest replacing your doctor; they suggest that the asymmetry of information between patient and clinician is narrowing, and that informed patients who arrive with AI-generated questions are likely to receive better care.

The Edit
Is the world becoming more predictable?

The piece makes a quietly radical argument: that AI and larger datasets are not just improving forecasts but exposing patterns in human behaviour, weather, and markets that were previously invisible — raising the question of whether unpredictability itself was always partly a measurement failure rather than genuine randomness. Worth reading slowly on a Sunday morning before the week's noise begins.

The smartphone era created an attention crisis. Slowtech is fixing it

A useful survey of the growing 'slowtech' movement — products and design philosophies deliberately built to reduce stimulation rather than maximise engagement — at a moment when the cultural mood is shifting decisively against the attention economy. The concrete detail is that the most effective interventions are not digital detoxes but friction by design: tools that make distraction slightly harder rather than requiring willpower to resist it.

Sequoia's Luciana Lixandru on Europe's tech 'act two'

Luciana Lixandru, who co-leads Sequoia's early-stage investments globally, argues that Europe is entering a second act in tech — one where the infrastructure, talent density, and funding networks built over the past decade finally compound into genuine category-defining companies rather than acqui-hire candidates. For anyone working in or around London's tech ecosystem, her read on where Sequoia is placing bets in Europe is worth more than any market report.
