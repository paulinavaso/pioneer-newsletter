01  The Week in Brief AI · Geopolitics · Tech · Funding

Five stories from the intersection of AI, geopolitics and the technology economy — curated and commented.

The US export ban on advanced AI models is reshaping alliances, accelerating sovereign AI strategies worldwide, and exposing a deepening rift between Washington's commercial interests and its allies' security needs. Meanwhile, the talent war at the frontier of AI intensified as Nobel laureate John Jumper left Google DeepMind for Anthropic, and China demonstrated it can now build world-leading supercomputers without a single American chip. The week made one thing clear: compute is the new geopolitical currency.

US export ban on Anthropic's AI models further strains alliances

— Al Jazeera English

The Trump administration has blocked allied nations from accessing Anthropic's most advanced AI models, including Claude Mythos and Fable 5, as part of a broader US AI export control regime. The move has prompted allies to accelerate sovereign AI programmes and call for greater technological self-reliance.

Why it matters

Washington is treating frontier AI as a strategic weapon rather than a shared asset, forcing even close allies to conclude that dependence on US AI infrastructure is a national security liability.

The London lens

Britain has been directly cut off, with commentators warning the country risks becoming a 'third-rate power' in AI capability terms. The episode has sharpened the case for UK investment in homegrown frontier AI and strengthened arguments for European AI sovereignty initiatives.

China takes the supercomputer crown without US chips

— The Next Web

A supercomputer in Shenzhen has topped the global Top500 rankings for the first time since 2017, running entirely on domestic Chinese silicon without a single component from Nvidia, AMD, or Intel. The achievement signals that US export controls, while painful, have not stopped China from reaching the computational frontier.

Why it matters

It demolishes the assumption underpinning Western chip export policy — that denying China advanced semiconductors would cap its AI and computing ambitions.

Tech context

Supercomputing performance is measured in FLOPS (floating-point operations per second). Topping the Top500 list, which ranks the world's most powerful systems, means China has built a machine that outperforms every other publicly benchmarked system using chips designed and manufactured domestically — a feat that requires mastery across chip design, manufacturing, and systems engineering simultaneously.

DeepSeek instructs investors against poaching employees as it closes $7.4 billion funding round

— Crypto Briefing

DeepSeek founder Liang Wenfeng told prospective investors in a four-hour virtual meeting that a condition of participating in the company's $7.4 billion maiden fundraise is a binding no-poach agreement covering its research staff. The condition reflects how seriously China's leading AI lab views talent retention as a competitive moat.

Why it matters

DeepSeek treating its researchers as a non-negotiable protected asset — and investors accepting that condition to get access — tells you everything about where real AI leverage now sits: in the people, not the capital.

Groq's $650M raise: rebuilding after the Nvidia deal

— The Next Web

Groq has raised $650 million to rebuild itself as an AI inference cloud, six months after Nvidia acquired its founder and bought out early investors. The round is a direct bet that purpose-built AI inference chips can outcompete Nvidia's general-purpose GPUs on speed and cost for specific workloads.

Why it matters

As AI inference costs become the dominant constraint on enterprise AI adoption, a well-funded alternative to Nvidia's GPU monopoly on inference hardware has significant implications for pricing power across the entire AI stack.

Tech context

Inference is the process of running a trained AI model to generate outputs — as distinct from training, which builds the model in the first place. Purpose-built inference chips can be dramatically faster and cheaper than GPUs for this specific task, but they sacrifice the flexibility that makes GPUs dominant across the broader market.

Menlo Ventures raises $3bn on the back of one Anthropic bet

— The Next Web

Menlo Ventures has raised $3 billion, the largest fund in its 50-year history, substantially underwritten by its early Anthropic stake which is now valued at around $14 billion. The raise marks a new era in venture fundraising where a single AI position can define an entire firm's trajectory.

Why it matters

When one bet in one company returns enough to anchor a $3 billion fund, it confirms that AI is not just a sector — it is rewriting the economics of venture capital itself.

Watch This

China's tightening scrutiny of indium exports — a metal critical for high-speed AI chip manufacturing — has attracted little attention this week, but if it follows the pattern of earlier Chinese critical mineral restrictions on gallium and germanium, it could become a significant chokepoint for Western AI hardware supply chains within six months.

02  Minds Worth Following Essays · Long reads · Podcasts

The sharpest thing written, said or published this week — on AI, ideas, or anything worth thinking about.

Vox

Who gave AI companies the right to build the future?

Drawing a parallel to J. Robert Oppenheimer's post-Manhattan Project reckoning, the piece asks whether AI companies have appropriated the right to shape civilisation without democratic mandate. The central argument is that the Oppenheimer analogy cuts both ways: like atomic scientists, today's AI researchers believe they are building something inevitable, which becomes a self-fulfilling justification for moving fast regardless of consequences. The piece forces the question of who, if anyone, is actually authorising these decisions on behalf of the public.

Tim.blog

The Tim Ferriss Show: Sebastian Mallaby on the race to superintelligence

Sebastian Mallaby, biographer of Demis Hassabis and a senior fellow at the Council on Foreign Relations, draws on interviews with over 100 AI insiders to map the culture, theology, and competitive dynamics inside the leading AI labs. His central insight is that the people building frontier AI have developed something close to a belief system — a conviction that superintelligence is both imminent and redemptive — which shapes decisions in ways that conventional corporate or scientific incentives cannot explain. For anyone trying to understand why the labs behave as they do, this is more illuminating than any earnings call.

Harvard Business Review

Employees aren't questioning AI advice enough

A new study finds that people do not merely accept AI recommendations passively — they actively avoid seeking information that might complicate or contradict what the AI has told them, particularly when the decision involves moral discomfort. The mechanism is not laziness but motivated reasoning: AI outputs provide cover, and additional information threatens that cover. For organisations deploying AI in consequential decisions, this is a more dangerous failure mode than hallucination.

03  Staying Human Body · Mind · The Analogue World

Your health, your attention, your life off the screen. Evidence-led and never preachy.

The Practice

The case for a hard stop: why ending your workday at a fixed time improves sleep quality and next-day cognition

Research consistently shows that the transition from work to rest is not passive — it requires an active buffer, and without one, cognitive arousal from unfinished tasks disrupts both sleep onset and slow-wave sleep quality. The mechanism is what psychologists call the Zeigarnik effect: incomplete tasks occupy working memory involuntarily, keeping the prefrontal cortex active when it should be winding down. A structured shutdown ritual — writing a brief list of tomorrow's priorities, then a deliberate verbal declaration that work is done — has been shown to interrupt this loop and reduce pre-sleep rumination. For professionals whose work has no natural end point, this is not a productivity hack but a neurological necessity.

The White House is making up its AI rules in real time

Wired's investigation into the Anthropic export control saga finds that Anthropic cannot distribute its most advanced models not because it broke a clearly defined rule, but because the rules themselves do not yet exist in any coherent form — the administration is improvising enforcement as it goes, leaving companies and allies unable to plan.

The global race for compute

A clear-eyed survey of how the US export ban on Anthropic models has become the catalyst that finally forced governments worldwide to treat sovereign AI infrastructure as a non-negotiable strategic priority — the piece maps which nations are moving fast and which are still hoping the problem goes away.
