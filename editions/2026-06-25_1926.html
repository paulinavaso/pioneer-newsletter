01  The Week in Brief AI · Geopolitics · Tech · Funding

Five stories from the intersection of AI, geopolitics and the technology economy — curated and commented.

This week, the AI infrastructure race hit the physical economy hard: memory chip shortages are repricing consumer electronics, Micron posted a 15-fold profit surge, and OpenAI revealed its first custom silicon. Meanwhile, the geopolitics of chips escalated as Europe pushed back against Washington's export controls, and Anthropic found itself in a direct confrontation with Alibaba over illicit access to its models.

Anthropic accuses Alibaba of obtaining illicit access to Claude

— Financial Times

Anthropic has accused Alibaba of using fake accounts to systematically extract the capabilities of its Claude AI model, bypassing normal access controls. The allegation marks a significant escalation in tensions between US AI labs and Chinese technology companies.

Why it matters

It signals that the competition between US and Chinese AI is now playing out through corporate espionage as much as direct model development, raising the stakes for every major lab's security posture.

Tech context

What Anthropic describes as 'extracting capabilities' likely refers to model distillation — a technique where a smaller or rival model is trained by querying a more powerful one at scale, effectively transferring its knowledge without building it from scratch.

Europe is pushing back on Washington's chip war

— TechCrunch

European officials and industry leaders are resisting US pressure to extend semiconductor export controls, with the proposed MATCH Act threatening to restrict sales of older deep ultraviolet lithography tools to China. ASML's CEO has noted that what China can currently buy is already decade-old technology, making the proposed restrictions economically damaging to Europe while offering limited strategic benefit.

Why it matters

If Europe and the US cannot align on export control policy, the Western technology coalition fractures precisely when coherent strategic coordination matters most.

The London lens

The UK's position outside the EU means it must independently calibrate its own semiconductor export policy, and how London aligns with Washington versus Brussels on this issue will define its credibility as a serious technology partner to both.

OpenAI unveils its first custom chip, built by Broadcom

— TechCrunch

OpenAI has revealed its first proprietary processor, named Jalapeño, designed specifically for inference workloads and built in partnership with Broadcom. The move is a direct attempt to reduce the company's dependence on Nvidia and lower the unit economics of running its models at scale.

Why it matters

When the world's most prominent AI lab builds its own silicon, it accelerates the fragmentation of the chip market and signals that inference efficiency — not just training power — is becoming the critical competitive battleground.

Tech context

Inference is the process of running a trained AI model to generate responses, as opposed to the initial training phase. Custom inference chips can dramatically cut cost-per-query, which matters enormously for a company serving hundreds of millions of users.

General Intuition's $2.3B bet that video games can train AI agents for the real world

— TechCrunch

General Intuition has raised $320 million at a $2.3 billion valuation to scale AI systems trained on millions of hours of gameplay footage, arguing that the action-and-consequence structure of games can teach AI something closer to intuitive, adaptive reasoning. The company believes this approach can produce agents capable of operating in unpredictable real-world environments in ways that language-model-based agents currently cannot.

Why it matters

If gameplay data proves to be a meaningful training substrate for real-world AI agents, it opens an entirely new data economy and reframes gaming as critical AI infrastructure rather than a peripheral industry.

NHS England admits key data does not prove Palantir's effectiveness

— Financial Times

NHS England has admitted that it cannot attribute performance improvements to its £330 million contract with Palantir, adding a caveat to previously positive assessments of the data platform's impact. The admission raises serious questions about how public sector AI contracts are evaluated and whether outcomes are being measured rigorously enough before large sums are committed.

Why it matters

With governments worldwide committing billions to AI contracts for public services, the NHS case becomes a test of whether procurement accountability can keep pace with the speed of AI adoption.

The London lens

This directly implicates the UK government's approach to AI in the public sector and will sharpen scrutiny of future NHS technology deals at a moment when the new administration is under pressure to demonstrate that AI spending delivers measurable value.

Watch This

Kalshi, the predictions market platform, is in talks to raise at a $40 billion valuation — if that round closes, it will accelerate the legitimisation of prediction markets as a mainstream financial instrument, with significant implications for how institutions price geopolitical and economic risk within 12 months.

02  Minds Worth Following Essays · Long reads · Podcasts

The sharpest thing written, said or published this week — on AI, ideas, or anything worth thinking about.

Financial Times

What if ideas aren't getting harder to find, after all?

The piece challenges the widely accepted economic thesis that innovation is slowing because all the easy ideas have been found. The central argument is that large companies may have become systematically better at concealing the fruits of their research — through IP strategy, vertical integration, and data moats — making innovation appear scarcer than it actually is. The implication is that the productivity paradox may be a measurement failure as much as a genuine economic one.

Financial Times

How much compute does the world really need?

This piece argues that the AI industry's addiction to scale is running into a fundamental ceiling: more compute does not solve the accuracy problem at the heart of large language models. The author makes the case that the current infrastructure spending race is partially a confidence game — a way of deferring the harder question of whether the underlying architecture can ever be reliable enough for the use cases being sold to enterprise customers. For anyone allocating budget to AI deployments, it is a necessary corrective to the hype cycle.

Financial Times

Why aren't more companies adopting AI?

Despite years of headlines about AI transformation, adoption inside most organisations remains shallow. The piece identifies the barrier not as technology cost or availability but as organisational structure — specifically, the gap between the teams that evaluate AI tools and the operational teams who would need to change their workflows to use them. The argument is that AI adoption is fundamentally a change management problem dressed up as a technology problem.

Quote of the week

“Scale cannot solve AI's fundamental problem with accuracy. We are building an infrastructure expecting a breakthrough that may not arrive on the timeline the investment requires.”

— Financial Times analysis, 'How much compute does the world really need?', June 2026
03  Staying Human Body · Mind · The Analogue World

Your health, your attention, your life off the screen. Evidence-led and never preachy.

The Practice

Rethink your recovery window, not just your sleep duration

Emerging research on cognitive recovery distinguishes between total sleep time and the quality of the transition between wakefulness and sleep — the period known as sleep onset. For professionals with high cognitive loads, the evidence suggests that shortening the time between stopping screens and attempting sleep, ideally to under 20 minutes, has a more measurable effect on next-day executive function than adding an extra 30 minutes of total sleep time. The mechanism is cortisol regulation: bright light and social stimulation in the hour before bed keep cortisol elevated, which delays the onset of deep slow-wave sleep regardless of when you lie down. A consistent wind-down protocol — low light, no reactive tasks, and a fixed pre-sleep anchor such as reading or a short walk — is the intervention most consistently supported by sleep researchers for people whose professional lives make early bedtimes structurally impossible.

Via Why We Sleep, Matthew Walker / Huberman Lab  ·  Matthew Walker, UC Berkeley Centre for Human Sleep Science
The Edit
The reason China is building most of the world's robots

China's workforce is projected to fall from roughly 900 million today to 300 million by the end of the century — a demographic collapse so severe that humanoid robots are now official state infrastructure policy, not a tech curiosity. The piece reframes the global robotics race as a response to demography rather than ambition, which changes how you read every subsequent headline about Chinese industrial automation.

How AI is transforming natural disaster prediction

The piece shows how catastrophe scientists are using AI to move beyond physics-based models that cap out at known variables, enabling insurers to price risk for compound disasters — floods triggered by fires, storms amplified by urban heat — that conventional actuarial models cannot handle. The practical implication is that insurance pricing for climate-exposed assets is about to become far more granular and, for many properties, far more expensive.

What Ambani's deep-tech ambitions reveal about India's AI limits

Despite Reliance's enormous capital and India's engineering talent base, the piece argues that India's AI ambitions are structurally constrained by a shortage of frontier researchers, a weak domestic chip supply chain, and a regulatory environment that makes large-scale data aggregation difficult. Ambani's bets are read here not as evidence of India's AI arrival but as a private-sector attempt to compensate for what the state has failed to build.
