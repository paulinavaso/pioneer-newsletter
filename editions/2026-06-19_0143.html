01  The Week in Brief AI · Geopolitics · Tech · Funding

Five stories from the intersection of AI, geopolitics and the technology economy — curated and commented.

The G7 summit this week crystallised the central tension of the AI age: world leaders want American AI infrastructure but fear the geopolitical leverage that comes with it. Meanwhile, the inference economy is accelerating — billions are moving into AI infrastructure plays from chips to world models — while the human cost is landing visibly at Accenture, Samsung, and in junior job specs across every industry.

Anthropic chief tells G7 leaders to 'resist the temptation to splinter' over AI

— Financial Times

Dario Amodei addressed G7 leaders this week calling for international cooperation on AI governance rather than fragmentation along national lines. He was backed by Sam Altman, even as French President Macron and Indian PM Modi raised alarms that the US could cut off allied access to American AI overnight — a fear the recent Anthropic blackout made viscerally real.

Why it matters

The G7 is becoming the primary arena for AI governance, and the gap between US providers and dependent allies is now a live geopolitical vulnerability, not a theoretical one.

The London lens

The UK, as a G7 member and host of the original AI Safety Summit at Bletchley, has positioned itself as the neutral convener in exactly this debate — but its leverage depends on allies not going bilateral with Washington first.

Trump is taking a page out of China's sovereign AI playbook

— Financial Times

Governments have long protected strategic industries through tariffs and subsidies, but a new pattern is emerging: states are taking equity stakes in AI companies and treating AI infrastructure as a sovereign asset. The Trump administration's approach mirrors Beijing's playbook more closely than it does traditional Western market policy.

Why it matters

When both Washington and Beijing treat AI as a state-owned strategic asset rather than a market product, every other government faces pressure to pick a side or build its own — there is no neutral ground left.

The London lens

The UK government's reluctance to take direct equity positions in domestic AI champions is becoming a structural disadvantage; without a sovereign AI posture, British companies risk being absorbed into American or Chinese supply chains rather than anchoring a third pole.

Amazon backs AI start-up developing models to simulate physical world

— Financial Times

Odyssey has closed a $310 million funding round backed by Amazon, Nvidia, and AMD at a $1.45 billion valuation. The company builds world models — AI systems that simulate the physical environment rather than generate text — and has positioned itself as the leading independent bet on the technology.

Why it matters

World models are the infrastructure layer that makes robotics, autonomous vehicles, and physical AI agents viable, meaning this funding round is less about one startup and more about who controls the simulation layer of the physical economy.

Tech context

World models differ from large language models in that they learn to predict how physical environments evolve over time — what happens when objects interact, how spaces change — rather than predicting the next word. They are the missing piece between AI reasoning and AI acting in the real world.

Accenture shares fall to lowest since 2017 as AI threat mounts

— Financial Times

Accenture's stock fell to its lowest level since 2017 as investors concluded that AI is not a tailwind for the IT consultancy but a structural threat to its billable-hours model. The same dynamic is visible at Samsung, where AI is boosting chip revenue but cutting IT headcount sharply.

Why it matters

Accenture is a bellwether for the global professional services industry — when its model breaks, it signals a fundamental shift in how large organisations buy and deploy technology expertise.

The London lens

The UK's professional services sector — the largest in Europe — faces the same structural pressure; London-based consultancies and system integrators are quietly repricing their workforce mix, and the next 18 months will determine which firms adapt and which shrink.

AI inference startup Baseten reportedly raising $1.5B months after its last mega-round

— TechCrunch

Baseten is reportedly close to closing a $1.5 billion round at a $13 billion valuation, just months after its previous large fundraise. The company sits in the inference infrastructure layer — the part of the AI stack that serves model outputs to real users — which has become the hottest capital destination in tech.

Why it matters

The velocity of capital into inference infrastructure signals that the industry has concluded the model layer is commoditising fast, and the real margin will be captured by whoever owns the serving and delivery stack.

Tech context

Inference is the process of running a trained AI model to generate outputs for users — distinct from training, which builds the model in the first place. As models become cheaper to train, the cost and speed of inference is increasingly where commercial advantage is won or lost.

Watch This

JPMorgan and Goldman Sachs have both now blocked Anthropic's Claude for staff in Hong Kong — a quiet but concrete signal that US AI export controls and geopolitical pressure are beginning to reshape which AI tools can be used where, a dynamic that could accelerate rapidly across Asian financial centres over the next six months.

02  Minds Worth Following Essays · Long reads · Podcasts

The sharpest thing written, said or published this week — on AI, ideas, or anything worth thinking about.

TechCrunch

World leaders want American AI. They just don't want America to be able to turn it off.

The piece argues that the G7 summit exposed a dependency problem that allied governments have largely avoided naming in public: they have built critical infrastructure on American AI platforms that Washington could shut off unilaterally. Macron and Modi's interventions were not abstract concerns — the recent episode where Anthropic restricted access to certain markets made the theoretical risk suddenly concrete. The piece frames this as the defining tension of the next decade of AI geopolitics: adoption and dependence are the same thing.

Financial Times

Tech's private subsea cables are a threat to everyone else

American hyperscalers — Google, Meta, Microsoft, Amazon — have been quietly building their own private undersea cable networks, bypassing the shared infrastructure that the rest of the internet depends on. The piece argues this is not merely a commercial efficiency play but a national security risk for every country that is not the United States: if the companies that own the physical data highways also control the AI running on top of them, sovereign digital policy becomes largely theoretical. The argument is that the infrastructure layer is the power layer, and most governments have already ceded it.

Financial Times

How AI is 'senior-ising' junior roles

The piece examines a structural shift in hiring: AI is eliminating the entry-level task work that used to be how junior employees learned their craft, so employers are now expecting new recruits to arrive as decision-makers and managers of AI outputs rather than apprentices. This is not straightforwardly good news — the pipeline of experience that produced senior professionals was built on doing the grunt work first. The central question the piece raises is whether we are training a generation of managers who have never learned what they are managing.

Quote of the week

03  Staying Human Body · Mind · The Analogue World

Your health, your attention, your life off the screen. Evidence-led and never preachy.

The Practice

The case for a hard stop: why professionals need a daily cognitive off-ramp

Research consistently shows that the brain's default mode network — responsible for consolidating memory, processing emotion, and generating creative insight — only activates during genuine rest, not passive scrolling or back-to-back calls. For professionals in high-decision-load environments, the absence of a deliberate end-of-day transition ritual is not merely a wellbeing issue but a cognitive performance one: without it, the prefrontal cortex remains in a low-grade activated state that impairs next-day decision quality. The prescription is straightforward — a fixed 15-minute wind-down protocol (a short walk, analogue writing, or a non-stimulating routine) that signals to the nervous system the working day has ended. The evidence base here is strongest for consistency over method: it is doing the same thing at the same time that creates the physiological off-ramp, not any particular activity.

Via Financial Times  ·  Andrew Huberman, Stanford Neuroscience
The Edit
Is the world becoming more predictable?

The piece argues that larger datasets and more powerful AI models are allowing scientists and analysts to detect patterns that were previously invisible — from weather systems to financial contagion — but that prediction and control are not the same thing, and the illusion of predictability may be as dangerous as uncertainty itself.

The smartphone era created an attention crisis. Slowtech is fixing it

A growing category of products and apps is being built on a single premise — that people want less stimulation, not more — and is finding serious commercial traction among exactly the professional demographic who adopted smartphones earliest and feel the cost of that most acutely.

Tech Exchange: Luciana Lixandru on 'act two' for European tech

Sequoia's global co-lead for early-stage investment argues that European founders have spent a decade being told they build slower and think smaller — and that the conditions have now materially shifted, with sovereign AI policy, defence tech, and climate infrastructure creating categories where European companies have genuine structural advantages over Silicon Valley.
