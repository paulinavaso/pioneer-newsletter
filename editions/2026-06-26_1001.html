01  The Week in Brief AI · Geopolitics · Tech · Funding

Five stories from the intersection of AI, geopolitics and the technology economy — curated and commented.

Two forces are colliding this week: memory chips have become the economy's most powerful chokepoint — Apple's 20% price rises and Micron's 15-fold profit surge tell the same story from opposite ends — while Washington is now actively managing AI model releases, asking OpenAI to stagger GPT-5.6 to vet users. Beneath both headlines, the deeper question is whether the infrastructure bets being made now — in India, in neocloud networking, in custom silicon — will determine who actually controls AI in five years.

Apple raises MacBook and iPad prices by 20% as AI memory shortage bites

— Financial Times

Apple has raised prices on MacBooks and iPads by 20%, blaming an 'unprecedented' shortage and cost of AI-driven memory chips. The announcement wiped $263bn from Apple's market capitalisation in a single session.

Why it matters

When Apple raises consumer prices due to a chip input shortage, the AI infrastructure build-out has moved from an industry story to a macroeconomic one — ordinary consumers are now paying the premium.

Tech context

The shortage centres on high-bandwidth memory (HBM), a stacked chip design that sits directly alongside AI processors to feed them data fast enough. AI training and inference demand for HBM has grown faster than manufacturers can expand capacity, squeezing supply available for consumer devices.

The London lens

UK consumers buying Apple hardware will pay these increases on top of existing sterling-denominated pricing, at a moment when the Bank of England is watching import-driven inflation closely.

Trump administration tells OpenAI to stagger release of GPT-5.6 over safety concerns

— Financial Times

The US Treasury, Commerce Department, and other federal offices have asked OpenAI to release its newest model, GPT-5.6, to a select group of partners rather than the general public, citing the need to vet users first. It is the first known instance of the Trump administration directly intervening in the timing of an AI model launch.

Why it matters

A White House capable of slowing frontier model releases has effectively inserted government as a gatekeeper in the AI race, a structural shift with implications for every lab planning a public launch.

The London lens

The UK's AI minister Kanishka Narayan argued this week that the UK must use specialisation and research to carve out leverage — but if Washington is now rationing access to frontier models, that leverage depends on whether British institutions are among the vetted few.

DeepSeek plans hiring spree in escalation of China's AI talent war

— Financial Times

DeepSeek is advertising a wave of new roles focused on commercialising its frontier research, suggesting the company is moving beyond pure research into product and revenue generation. The hiring push signals a new phase in China's AI ambitions, with DeepSeek positioning itself as a commercial force, not just a technical one.

Why it matters

DeepSeek shifting from research lab to commercial operation is the moment China's AI capability becomes a direct competitive threat to OpenAI and Anthropic in global markets, not just a geopolitical signal.

OpenAI unveils its first custom chip, built by Broadcom

— TechCrunch

OpenAI has revealed its first purpose-built processor, named Jalapeño, designed specifically for its inference workloads and manufactured in partnership with Broadcom. The chip is OpenAI's clearest move yet to reduce its dependence on Nvidia hardware.

Why it matters

If OpenAI can run its own inference on custom silicon at scale, it fundamentally changes the cost structure of AI deployment and weakens Nvidia's grip on the industry's most critical supply chain.

Tech context

Inference is the process of running a trained AI model to generate outputs — it is distinct from training and accounts for a growing majority of AI compute spend. Custom inference chips can be tuned precisely to a single company's model architecture, making them cheaper and faster than general-purpose GPUs for that specific task.

Anthropic accuses Alibaba of largest Claude cloning attack on record

— Ars Technica

Anthropic has alleged that Alibaba used 25,000 fake accounts to conduct 28.8 million exchanges with Claude in what the company is calling a systematic attempt to steal its model's capabilities. Anthropic is demanding Alibaba face consequences, framing the attack as a deliberate act carried out in defiance of the Trump administration.

Why it matters

If substantiated, this is the most brazen known example of a Chinese tech company using automated scraping to extract proprietary AI capability from a US rival — and it raises immediate questions about how AI labs can protect their models at the inference layer.

Tech context

Model distillation allows a smaller or less capable model to learn by systematically querying a more powerful one and training on its outputs. Done at sufficient scale — tens of millions of exchanges — it can meaningfully transfer capability without access to the original model's weights.

Watch This

Micron's 15-fold profit surge and its forecast of sustained memory demand is a leading indicator: if HBM capacity remains constrained through Q3, Apple's 20% price rises will not be the last consumer-facing price shock tied to AI infrastructure.

·

Anthropic's Claude is gaining ground among paying consumers despite ChatGPT's commanding free-tier lead — if that trend holds through Q3, it will force a reckoning about whether OpenAI's IPO valuation is built on the right user cohort.

02  Minds Worth Following Essays · Long reads · Podcasts

The sharpest thing written, said or published this week — on AI, ideas, or anything worth thinking about.

Financial Times

How the DeepMind mafia brought the AI boom to London

The piece traces how a generation of researchers who passed through DeepMind have seeded a cluster of AI companies across London, creating something that looks, from the outside, like a genuine tech ecosystem. But the central question it poses is harder: whether London can be more than a talent farm for US capital, or whether the structural incentives — acquisition exits, US VC dominance, dollar-denominated ambition — mean British AI will always be absorbed before it scales.

Financial Times

How much compute does the world really need?

The argument here is that scaling compute is not a path to solving AI's core reliability problem: more processing power cannot fix the fundamental tendency of large models to hallucinate or reason incorrectly in high-stakes domains. The piece challenges the prevailing assumption that the AI race is essentially a race to acquire more GPUs, suggesting that the labs betting billions on scale may be running hard in the wrong direction.

Financial Times

How to win at AI if you're not the US or China, with AI minister Kanishka Narayan

The UK's AI minister makes the case that mid-sized countries cannot win a compute arms race and should not try — the path to relevance runs through research excellence, regulatory credibility, and domain specialisation in areas like life sciences and finance. What the interview surfaces, between the lines, is a tension the minister does not fully resolve: specialisation is a viable strategy only if frontier models remain accessible, and Washington has just demonstrated it is willing to restrict that access.

Quote of the week

“Scale cannot solve AI's fundamental problem with accuracy. More compute does not make a model that reasons incorrectly reason correctly — it makes it more fluently wrong.”

— Financial Times analysis, 'How much compute does the world really need?', 25 June 2026
03  Staying Human Body · Mind · The Analogue World

Your health, your attention, your life off the screen. Evidence-led and never preachy.

The Practice

The case for a weekly analogue morning — and what the evidence actually says about it

Researchers studying cognitive restoration find that extended periods without digital input — no notifications, no screen-based decisions — allow the prefrontal cortex to recover from the sustained attentional load of knowledge work. The effect is not merely subjective: response inhibition, working memory, and creative problem-solving all measure higher after unstructured, low-stimulus time than after passive screen-based rest. For professionals running on back-to-back decisions, the implication is that the Sunday morning you spend reading a physical paper or walking without a podcast is not wasted time — it is the recovery window that makes the following week's thinking sharper. The research suggests even 90 minutes of genuinely analogue time, once weekly, produces measurable cognitive benefit.

Via Attention Restoration Theory research literature, summarised in coverage this week of digital overload and cognitive performance  ·  Rachel and Stephen Kaplan, University of Michigan — originators of Attention Restoration Theory
The Edit
IBM's sub-1nm chip prototype — what it actually means for Moore's Law

IBM has built a prototype chip packing around 100 billion transistors into the area of a fingernail — twice the density of its 2021 record — using a nanosheet transistor architecture that sidesteps the physical limits that were supposed to end Moore's Law this decade. The practical takeaway: if this scales to manufacture, the energy efficiency gains arrive precisely when data centres are under acute pressure from AI workloads and European heat waves simultaneously.

Europe's heat wave and the grid — a dress rehearsal for a permanent problem

The piece explains how record temperatures are simultaneously spiking electricity demand and forcing some thermal power plants offline — because the rivers used to cool them are too warm — creating a pinch that exposes the grid's structural fragility at the exact moment AI data centres are adding enormous baseload demand. London professionals with any exposure to energy markets, infrastructure investment, or climate policy will find the mechanics here unusually clarifying.

Stripe, Anthropic, and OpenAI back a serious attempt to eliminate the common cold

The Collison brothers at Stripe are funding a new respiratory infection research programme backed by the AI labs — the argument being that rhinovirus and similar infections have been ignored precisely because they are ubiquitous, not because they are unsolvable. For anyone tracking where serious tech-adjacent philanthropic capital is flowing beyond climate and longevity, this is a notable new direction.
