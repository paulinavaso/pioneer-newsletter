01  The Week in Brief AI · Geopolitics · Tech · Funding

Five stories from the intersection of AI, geopolitics and the technology economy — curated and commented.

The US export ban on Anthropic's AI models is reshaping geopolitics faster than most observers anticipated, prompting allies to accelerate sovereign AI strategies while deepening distrust at the G7. Simultaneously, the global compute race intensified: China topped the supercomputer rankings without a single American chip, and Groq raised $650 million on the thesis that purpose-built silicon can still beat Nvidia. The week's underlying current is the same everywhere — control of AI infrastructure is becoming indistinguishable from national security.

US export ban on Anthropic's AI models strains alliances and accelerates sovereign AI strategies

— Al Jazeera English

The Trump administration's decision to block allied nations from accessing Anthropic's most advanced AI models has prompted anger and calls for self-reliance among governments that had assumed access was a given. G7 talks this week exposed a widening trust gap, with US strategic interests increasingly shaping the rules that were supposed to be global.

Why it matters

When the world's most capable AI becomes a geopolitical lever, every government without domestic frontier AI capability becomes structurally dependent — and structurally vulnerable.

The London lens

Britain was cut off without warning, and the political fallout has been conspicuously muted — a diplomatic crisis that commentators argue the Labour government has so far failed to treat with the urgency it demands. The episode makes the case for UK sovereign AI investment more concretely than any white paper has managed.

China tops supercomputer rankings for the first time since 2017 — using no US chips

— The Next Web

A supercomputer in Shenzhen has claimed the top spot in the global Top500 rankings, the first time China has held that position in nearly a decade. The more significant detail is the hardware: it runs entirely on domestic Chinese silicon, with no Nvidia, AMD, or Intel components.

Why it matters

Export controls were supposed to slow China's AI progress; this result suggests they have instead accelerated domestic chip development to a point where China can now compete at the absolute frontier without Western components.

Tech context

Supercomputer rankings measure raw computational throughput, and historically the top machines have relied on Nvidia's H100 or A100 GPUs. A machine reaching the summit on purely domestic silicon signals that China's chip ecosystem — long dismissed as years behind — has closed the gap at least in the high-performance computing segment.

DeepSeek closes $7.4 billion funding round with a no-poaching condition attached

— Crypto Briefing

DeepSeek's founder Liang Wenfeng told prospective investors in a four-hour virtual meeting that a condition of participating in the $7.4 billion fundraise is a commitment not to recruit the lab's staff. The firm's insistence on this clause reflects how central its research team is to its competitive edge.

Why it matters

The fact that China's most consequential AI lab views talent retention as important enough to make it a contractual term underlines that the global AI race is, at its core, a war for a few hundred exceptional researchers.

Groq raises $650 million to rebuild as an AI inference cloud after Nvidia acquired its founder

— The Next Web

Groq has confirmed a $650 million raise to reposition itself as a standalone AI inference cloud, six months after Nvidia paid out its early investors and hired away the company's founder. The round is a direct bet that purpose-built language processing chips can outperform general-purpose GPUs for inference workloads at scale.

Why it matters

If Groq's architecture delivers on cost and speed at scale, it represents a credible alternative to Nvidia's inference dominance at a moment when inference costs are becoming one of enterprise AI's biggest unsolved problems.

Tech context

Inference is the process of running a trained AI model to generate outputs — as distinct from training the model in the first place. It is where most of the real-world compute cost accumulates, and it is a different computational task from training, which is why chips optimised for inference can outperform general-purpose GPUs even if they lose on training benchmarks.

Menlo Ventures raises $3 billion — effectively on the back of a single Anthropic bet

— The Next Web

Menlo Ventures has closed its largest-ever fund at $3 billion, the firm's 50-year record, funded in significant part by returns from its early Anthropic investment now worth approximately $14 billion. The raise illustrates how concentrated the gains from the current AI cycle have become — and how much of the new venture capital being deployed traces back to a handful of winning bets.

Why it matters

When a single portfolio position underwrites an entire generation of a major fund, it concentrates capital formation in AI around a very narrow set of gatekeepers and compounds the advantage of those who got in earliest.

Watch This

China's scrutiny of indium exports — a metal essential for high-speed AI chip manufacturing — has received almost no coverage this week, but if it follows the pattern of earlier Chinese critical mineral restrictions, a formal export control announcement in the next quarter could disrupt AI chip supply chains more acutely than any Nvidia export ban.

02  Minds Worth Following Essays · Long reads · Podcasts

The sharpest thing written, said or published this week — on AI, ideas, or anything worth thinking about.

Vox

Who gave AI companies the right to build the future?

Using J. Robert Oppenheimer's 1954 security hearing as its frame, this piece asks a question that almost no one in the AI industry wants to sit with: by what democratic mandate are a handful of private companies making decisions that will shape civilisation? The central argument is that AI labs have adopted the posture of reluctant public servants while retaining the prerogatives of private actors, and that this contradiction is becoming harder to sustain as their models become consequential enough to be banned by governments. It is a genuinely uncomfortable read precisely because it does not have an easy answer.

Tim Ferriss Show

Sebastian Mallaby on the race to superintelligence, the religion of AI, and spotting breakthroughs early

Sebastian Mallaby, who has spent years interviewing more than a hundred AI insiders while writing the biography of Demis Hassabis, offers the clearest outsider-looking-in account of what actually drives the people building frontier AI. His core insight is that the culture inside leading labs has taken on the character of a religious movement — with genuine believers in both the transformative upside and the existential risk — and that this dual belief system is what makes the labs so hard to regulate from outside. The transcript is long but the density of specific insight per page is unusually high.

Harvard Business Review

Employees aren't questioning AI advice enough

A new study finds that people do not merely accept AI recommendations uncritically — they actively avoid seeking information that might complicate or contradict what the AI has told them, particularly when doing so would create moral discomfort. The mechanism is not laziness but motivated reasoning: if the AI has already handed you a convenient answer, looking for a second opinion feels like creating a problem you did not have. For anyone managing teams that rely on AI tools, this is a more serious organisational risk than the more obvious concern about AI hallucinations.

03  Staying Human Body · Mind · The Analogue World

Your health, your attention, your life off the screen. Evidence-led and never preachy.

The Practice

The case for deliberate information friction before making decisions

The Harvard Business Review study published this week identifies a specific cognitive trap that is becoming more common as AI tools proliferate: when an AI gives you a clear recommendation, most people not only accept it but actively avoid seeking additional information that might complicate it. The researchers call this motivated information avoidance, and it is distinct from ordinary laziness — it is a deliberate, if unconscious, choice to protect a convenient conclusion. The practical countermeasure is to build a brief structured pause into any AI-assisted decision: explicitly ask what the AI might be missing, and seek at least one contradicting data point before acting. For senior professionals whose decisions carry real consequences, the risk is not that AI is wrong — it is that it is confidently wrong in ways that feel like certainty.

The Edit
Google DeepMind and A24: a $75 million bet on AI filmmaking tools

DeepMind is paying A24 $75 million to develop AI tools for its filmmakers — a partnership that matters not because of the money but because A24 is the studio most associated with auteur-driven, low-budget work. If AI filmmaking tools take root here rather than at a Marvel factory, the creative implications are genuinely different.

The White House is making up its AI rules in real time

Wired reports that Anthropic still cannot distribute its most capable models weeks after running afoul of the Trump administration — and that no one, including the company, can say precisely what rule was broken. The piece reveals that the export control regime governing frontier AI is not a coherent framework but a series of ad hoc decisions, which creates a different kind of risk for every organisation building on top of these models.
