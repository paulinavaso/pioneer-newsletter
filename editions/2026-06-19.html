01  The Week in Brief AI · Geopolitics · Tech · Funding

Five stories from the intersection of AI, geopolitics and the technology economy — curated and commented.

This week, AI crossed three thresholds at once: it entered the courtroom as a scandal, the G7 as a diplomatic fault line, and the public markets as a capital-raising machine. The central tension is no longer whether AI will reshape institutions, but who controls it, who profits, and who bears the cost when it goes wrong.

Anthropic chief tells G7 leaders to 'resist the temptation to splinter' over AI

— Financial Times

Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei addressed G7 leaders calling for international cooperation on AI governance, with rival Sam Altman offering rare public support for the position. The appeal came as French President Macron and Indian PM Modi separately raised fears that the US could cut off access to American AI systems overnight — a concern made concrete by recent Anthropic access restrictions in some markets.

Why it matters

The fracture between sovereign AI ambitions and US platform dependency is now a live diplomatic issue, not a theoretical one, and the G7 is the arena where the terms will be set.

The London lens

The UK sits awkwardly between its ambition to be an AI bridge between the US and Europe and its actual leverage, which is limited — the G7 debate will force a clearer choice on which side of the dependency argument London lands.

Trump is taking a page out of China's sovereign AI playbook

— Financial Times

Governments have long protected strategic industries through tariffs and subsidies, but a new phase has begun in which states are becoming direct shareholders in AI companies. The US under Trump is mirroring China's model of state-backed national champions, blurring the line between private enterprise and geopolitical infrastructure.

Why it matters

When the world's two largest economies converge on state-directed AI investment, every other government faces a forced choice between sovereignty and access to the best technology.

The London lens

The UK's arms-length approach to AI investment — backing through grants and compute access rather than equity — looks increasingly thin against this model, and the debate inside DSIT about whether Britain needs a sovereign AI champion will intensify.

Amazon backs AI start-up developing models to simulate physical world

— Financial Times

Odyssey has raised $310 million from Amazon, Nvidia, and AMD at a $1.45 billion valuation to build world models — AI systems that simulate physical environments rather than just process language. The round signals that the next frontier of AI investment is moving beyond large language models toward systems that can reason about and predict the physical world.

Why it matters

World models are the foundational technology for robotics, autonomous vehicles, and scientific simulation, making this class of investment a bet on the next decade of AI applications rather than the current one.

Tech context

A world model is an AI system trained to understand and predict how physical environments behave over time — think of it as a simulator of reality rather than a generator of text. Unlike LLMs, which predict the next word, world models predict the next state of a physical system, which is essential for training robots and autonomous vehicles without requiring billions of real-world hours.

Rape convictions under review after UK detective allegedly used AI chatbot for paperwork

— Financial Times

Derbyshire Police is investigating whether an officer used an AI chatbot to generate or manipulate paperwork in a way that influenced court outcomes in rape cases. Convictions are now under review, making this one of the first cases in which AI misuse in law enforcement has directly triggered a criminal justice crisis.

Why it matters

This is the scenario AI governance frameworks have been designed to prevent, and its arrival in a UK police force will accelerate calls for mandatory audit trails on AI use in public institutions.

The London lens

UK courts and police forces have no uniform policy on AI tool usage, and this case will almost certainly force the Home Office and Ministry of Justice to act — UK legal AI companies including Luminance will face new scrutiny alongside new opportunity.

AI inference startup Baseten reportedly raising $1.5B months after its last mega-round

— TechCrunch

Baseten is reported to be close to closing a $1.5 billion funding round at a $13 billion valuation, just months after its previous large raise. The round reflects surging demand for inference infrastructure — the compute layer that runs AI models in production — as enterprises move from experimenting with AI to deploying it at scale.

Why it matters

The speed of successive mega-rounds at the infrastructure layer confirms that the real AI gold rush right now is not in model building but in the plumbing that serves them.

Tech context

Inference is the process of running a trained AI model to produce outputs — it is what happens every time you send a message to ChatGPT or Claude. As enterprise AI deployments scale, inference costs and latency become the dominant operational challenge, which is why dedicated inference infrastructure companies are attracting capital at a pace that rivals the model labs themselves.

Watch This

JPMorgan Chase and Goldman Sachs have both now restricted Claude access for staff in Hong Kong — a quiet signal that Western banks are beginning to treat AI access as a regulatory and geopolitical compliance variable, not just a cost one, and that Asia-Pacific operations may diverge significantly from Western ones in AI tooling within the next two quarters.

02  Minds Worth Following Essays · Long reads · Podcasts

The sharpest thing written, said or published this week — on AI, ideas, or anything worth thinking about.

TechCrunch

World leaders want American AI. They just don't want America to be able to turn it off.

The piece argues that the Anthropic access blackout made viscerally real a fear that has been abstract until now: that dependence on US AI infrastructure is a strategic vulnerability for every other country. Macron and Modi's interventions at the G7 were not anti-AI positions but anti-dependency ones — they want the technology but not the kill switch. The piece frames this as the defining tension of the next phase of AI geopolitics: adoption versus sovereignty.

Financial Times

Tech's private subsea cables are a threat to everyone else

American hyperscalers — Google, Meta, Microsoft, Amazon — have quietly built their own private networks of undersea cables that now carry a substantial and growing share of global internet traffic. The piece argues this is not just a commercial advantage but a national security risk for every country whose data flows through infrastructure it does not control and cannot inspect. The argument extends the AI sovereignty debate into physical infrastructure: the dependency is not only on models and chips, but on the cables that carry the data itself.

Financial Times

How AI is 'senior-ising' junior roles

The piece examines a structural shift in hiring: AI has automated the entry-level tasks that used to constitute the first two years of a professional career, meaning employers now expect new recruits to perform the judgement-heavy work that previously took years to reach. This is not about job destruction but about the collapse of the learning ladder — the question it raises is where junior professionals now develop the tacit knowledge they used to acquire by doing the work AI has replaced.

03  Staying Human Body · Mind · The Analogue World

Your health, your attention, your life off the screen. Evidence-led and never preachy.

The Practice

AI diagnostic tools now match doctors — here is what that means for how you use both

Two new studies published this week show that AI health models matched or outperformed physicians across a range of diagnostic and treatment decisions. For professionals who rely heavily on their own cognitive performance, the practical implication is not that you should replace your doctor, but that AI tools are now a legitimate first-pass triage layer — particularly for symptoms you might otherwise dismiss or delay acting on. The evidence suggests these tools perform best when used to prompt a conversation with a clinician, not to conclude one. The broader point for demanding professionals is this: the same AI capability that is disrupting your industry is also giving you access to a standard of medical reasoning that previously required an expensive specialist and a long wait.
