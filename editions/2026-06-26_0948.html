01  The Week in Brief AI · Geopolitics · Tech · Funding

Five stories from the intersection of AI, geopolitics and the technology economy — curated and commented.

Two signals dominated this week: the AI memory crunch is now hitting consumer prices, with Apple raising MacBook and iPad costs by 20% as Micron posted a fifteen-fold profit surge, and the White House moved to slow OpenAI's latest model release — a reminder that government is now an active participant in how frontier AI reaches the market. Underneath both stories runs a deeper question about who controls the pace and direction of the AI build-out.

Apple raises prices 20% as AI memory crunch hits consumers

— Financial Times

Apple has raised MacBook and iPad prices by 20%, blaming an AI-driven shortage of high-bandwidth memory chips that wiped $263 billion from its market capitalisation in a single session. The news sent Asian markets sharply lower and added to fears that the cost of the AI infrastructure build-out is beginning to pass through to ordinary consumers.

Why it matters

The memory crunch is no longer an abstract supply-chain story — it is now visibly repricing consumer electronics and rattling equity markets globally.

Tech context

The shortage centres on high-bandwidth memory, a specialised chip architecture that stacks DRAM dies vertically to move data far faster than conventional memory — it is essential for AI inference at scale, and its supply is controlled by a handful of manufacturers.

The London lens

UK consumers buying Apple hardware will face the same 20% premium; for London's tech and creative sectors, which run heavily on Mac infrastructure, this is a direct operating cost increase arriving with little warning.

Trump administration tells OpenAI to stagger release of GPT 5.6

— TechCrunch

The US Treasury, commerce department and other government offices asked OpenAI to limit distribution of its newest model, GPT 5.6, to a select group of partners rather than releasing it publicly — a request the company appears to be honouring. It is the most direct instance yet of the executive branch intervening in the release schedule of a commercial AI model.

Why it matters

This sets a precedent for government as a gatekeeper of frontier model releases, with significant implications for how AI labs balance commercial timelines against national security vetting.

The London lens

The UK's AI minister Kanishka Narayan argued this week that specialisation and research can give Britain leverage in AI; a US government that now controls model release schedules makes that case simultaneously more urgent and more complicated for UK developers reliant on American frontier models.

DeepSeek plans hiring spree in escalation of China's AI talent war

— Financial Times

DeepSeek has advertised a significant number of new roles, with the job listings suggesting the company is pivoting from pure research toward commercialising its frontier models at scale. The move signals that China's most technically credible AI lab is entering a new phase — one focused on revenue and product rather than benchmark victories.

Why it matters

DeepSeek commercialising aggressively would intensify competition with OpenAI and Anthropic precisely as both prepare for IPOs and face renewed open-source pressure.

Anthropic says Alibaba used 25,000 accounts to clone Claude

— Ars Technica

Anthropic has alleged that Alibaba orchestrated the largest known model-cloning attack on record, deploying 25,000 accounts to conduct 28.8 million exchanges with Claude in an attempt to extract and replicate its capabilities. Anthropic is seeking punitive action, framing the attack as a deliberate corporate theft that defied the Trump administration.

Why it matters

If proven, this represents a new frontier in AI IP warfare — model capabilities extracted systematically through API abuse rather than through chips or code theft, and it will accelerate demands for legal frameworks that do not yet exist.

Tech context

Model distillation via API querying — using a more capable model's outputs to train a cheaper imitation — is a known technique; what Alibaba allegedly did is an industrial-scale version of this, designed to replicate Claude's behaviour without ever touching its weights.

OpenAI unveils its first custom chip, Jalapeño, built by Broadcom

— TechCrunch

OpenAI has unveiled Jalapeño, its first in-house processor, designed specifically for inference workloads and manufactured in partnership with Broadcom. The chip marks OpenAI's first concrete step toward reducing its dependence on Nvidia hardware for running its models at scale.

Why it matters

A credible custom silicon programme from OpenAI reshapes the competitive dynamics of the AI chip market and — if successful — could significantly reduce the cost and latency of serving frontier models, putting pressure on every other lab's unit economics.

Tech context

Inference chips differ from training chips in that they are optimised for running a finished model quickly and cheaply at high volume, rather than for the slow, power-intensive process of learning from data — this distinction matters enormously for profitability.

Watch This

Micron's fifteen-fold profit surge and its forecast of sustained memory demand is worth tracking as a leading indicator: if high-bandwidth memory supply remains constrained into Q4, the Apple price rises this week are a preview of broader hardware repricing across the industry.

·

Anthropic's Claude is gaining share among paying consumers despite ChatGPT's commanding lead — if that trend continues into the IPO window for both companies, it could materially shift how public markets price the difference between the two.

02  Minds Worth Following Essays · Long reads · Podcasts

The sharpest thing written, said or published this week — on AI, ideas, or anything worth thinking about.

Financial Times

How the DeepMind mafia brought the AI boom to London

The piece traces how alumni of DeepMind have seeded a generation of London AI startups, creating a talent network that rivals anything outside the Bay Area and a handful of European capitals. But the central tension the FT identifies is structural: London's ecosystem produces world-class research and early-stage companies, yet the capital and ambition required to scale them to global dominance consistently pulls founders and companies toward the US. The question the piece leaves open — and does not flinch from — is whether London is building an AI industry or a very good feeder system for one.

Financial Times

How to win at AI if you're not the US or China, with AI minister Kanishka Narayan

The UK's AI minister argues that mid-sized nations cannot compete with American or Chinese compute budgets and should not try to — the winning strategy is deep specialisation in specific verticals, strong research institutions, and regulatory environments that attract companies unwilling to operate under either Washington's or Beijing's rules. Narayan is notably honest about the constraints: the UK cannot build a frontier lab, but it can become the place where frontier labs want to deploy. The interview is more candid than most ministerial conversations about AI, and the distinction between being a player and being a platform is worth sitting with.

Financial Times

How much compute does the world really need?

The piece challenges the assumption underlying almost every AI investment thesis of the past three years: that scale — more compute, more data, more parameters — is the path to reliable, accurate AI. The argument is that scaling cannot solve AI's fundamental accuracy problem, because the errors that matter most are not ones that more training data fixes; they are structural, rooted in how these systems represent and reason about the world. For anyone evaluating AI vendor claims or approving AI budgets, this is a useful corrective to the dominant narrative.

Quote of the week

“Specialisation and research can give the UK leverage — we cannot out-spend the US or China on compute, but we can become the place where the companies building frontier AI want to operate.”

— Kanishka Narayan, UK AI Minister
03  Staying Human Body · Mind · The Analogue World

Your health, your attention, your life off the screen. Evidence-led and never preachy.

The Practice

The case for deliberate heat exposure — and its limits

Europe's record-breaking heat wave this week prompted MIT Technology Review to examine what extreme heat actually does to human physiology and to the infrastructure we depend on. For professionals, the relevant finding is straightforward: cognitive performance degrades measurably at sustained ambient temperatures above 27°C, well before the body registers distress. Sleep quality is the first casualty — core body temperature needs to drop by roughly one degree Celsius to initiate sleep, and a hot bedroom prevents this. The practical response for a demanding professional schedule is to prioritise sleep environment cooling above almost any other heat intervention: a cool bedroom matters more than a cold shower, and both matter more than dietary adjustments during a heat event.

The Edit
The DeepMind alumni network shaping London tech

A rare piece of honest, reported journalism about London's AI ecosystem that names the specific founders, companies, and structural disadvantages at play — and resists the boosterism that usually surrounds this subject.

Europe's heat wave and the fragility of the power grid

The article explains why extreme heat is paradoxically harder on electricity grids than extreme cold: cooling demand spikes simultaneously across entire regions, river temperatures rise and reduce the efficiency of thermal power plants, and solar panels — counterintuitively — lose efficiency as temperatures climb above their rated operating range.

IBM's sub-1nm chip and the next decade of Moore's Law

IBM has packed roughly 100 billion transistors onto a chip the size of a fingernail — twice the density of its previous record — using a new design approach that could extend meaningful performance gains for another decade at a moment when the AI industry is beginning to question whether hardware progress can keep pace with its ambitions.
